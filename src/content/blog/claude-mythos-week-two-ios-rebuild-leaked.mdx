Last week we wrote about [our first seven days with Claude Mythos Preview](/blog/we-tested-claude-mythos-preview-heres-what-happened) and how it found a two-year-old race condition in our journaling app. That post got more traffic than anything we've published this year. Turns out people are hungry for real-world Mythos stories that aren't just benchmark tables.

So here's week two. It was messier, more ambitious, and ended with someone leaking the whole model on Discord.

---

## Monday: The Refactoring Binge

After week one's security audit wins, we came back Monday morning like someone who just discovered power tools and now wants to renovate the entire house. Dangerous energy.

We started with **PromptKit**, our prompt engineering toolkit. PromptKit had grown organically over the past year, and the template engine was a mess — a 900-line `PromptTemplateManager` class that did everything from parsing to caching to rendering. The kind of file where you scroll for thirty seconds and still can't find the method you need.

We gave Mythos the full module and one instruction: "This class does too much. Propose an architecture that's more testable."

What came back wasn't just a refactor suggestion. It was a complete protocol-oriented redesign. Five protocols, seven conforming types, and a coordinator that wired them together. But here's what made our eyebrows go up — Mythos understood that PromptKit templates are used in a streaming context. Without us mentioning it. It designed the rendering pipeline with back-pressure support and cancellation tokens baked in, because it had apparently inferred from our `AsyncSequence` usage elsewhere in the codebase that we care about streaming performance.

We implemented the refactor in two days. The template engine went from 900 lines to 340 across the protocol files. Test coverage went from "we should really write some" to 87%.

---

## The Navigation Nightmare Fix

Tuesday, we pointed Mythos at **Renovise** — our renovation project management app. Renovise had a deep linking bug that had been haunting us since January. Users would tap a notification about a project update, the app would open, and then... nothing. Just the home screen staring back at them. The deep link would work fine if the app was already running, but cold launches dropped it somewhere between `SceneDelegate` and our navigation stack.

We'd spent two weekends trying to fix this. Two weekends of `print("DEBUG: deep link received")` scattered through the navigation code like bread crumbs in a forest fire.

Mythos took fourteen minutes. Again. (I'm starting to think fourteen minutes is its signature move.)

The problem was a race condition — because apparently every bug we write is a race condition — between our SwiftUI `NavigationPath` initialization and the scene connection lifecycle. The path was getting set before the root view had fully loaded its state from SwiftData, so the navigation push was silently swallowed.

Mythos proposed a coordinator pattern with a `NavigationIntent` queue. Deep links get queued as intents, and the coordinator only drains the queue once the root view signals readiness via a `ViewModifier`. Clean. Testable. And it fixed the bug on the first try, which is the kind of thing that makes you question whether you're even needed as a developer anymore.

---

## Wednesday: The GPT-5.5 Plot Twist

Right in the middle of our productive Mythos honeymoon, OpenAI walked in and dropped GPT-5.5.

The headline that made everyone lose their minds: **GPT-5.5 narrowly beats Claude Mythos Preview on Terminal-Bench 2.0.** The numbers were close — and honestly, Terminal-Bench measures something very specific about terminal-based agentic coding that doesn't necessarily reflect real-world capability. But the optics were brutal for Anthropic. Their "too dangerous to release" model just got edged out by a model anyone can sign up for.

Here's the thing nobody seemed to notice in the benchmark drama though: GPT-5.5 scored lower on SWE-bench Verified (89.2% vs Mythos's 93.9%) and significantly lower on CyberGym (71.4% vs 83.1%). The cybersecurity gap is still a canyon. But Terminal-Bench is flashy and terminal-based coding is what developers actually feel in their daily workflow, so that's what the headlines ran with.

We didn't switch to GPT-5.5 for our remaining Mythos access days. Not because of loyalty — because Mythos was actively making our iOS code better in ways that matter. Terminal-Bench measures whether a model can navigate a terminal and complete tasks. SWE-bench measures whether it can solve real software engineering problems. For our work, the latter is what counts.

---

## Thursday: The Leak

Then it got weird.

Fortune broke the story: a group of users in a private Discord had gained unauthorized access to Claude Mythos Preview. One of them was a third-party contractor for Anthropic who had access credentials. The group reportedly used leaked infrastructure details to locate the model and had been accessing it since announcement day.

The irony was so thick you could spread it on toast. Anthropic restricted Mythos to 40 vetted companies because the cybersecurity capabilities were too dangerous. Then a contractor and some Discord users guessed their way in within days.

Security researcher David Lindner's quote from the Fortune piece summed it up perfectly: "It was bound to happen." He went further: "If some group got access to it, it's already been breached by China."

This wasn't just embarrassing for Anthropic. It validated exactly the criticism we raised in our [first Mythos post](/blog/we-tested-claude-mythos-preview-heres-what-happened) — that restricting access to the biggest companies doesn't actually keep the model safe. It just keeps the people who'd use it responsibly from having it.

---

## Friday: The AISI Report

As if the week needed more plot points, the UK's AI Safety Institute published their independent evaluation of Mythos's cyber capabilities. And the numbers were sobering.

Mythos Preview became **the first AI model to complete a full 32-step corporate network attack simulation from start to finish.** The simulation — called "The Last Ones" — would take human security professionals an estimated 20 hours. Mythos completed it in 3 out of 10 attempts.

On expert-level Capture The Flag challenges, Mythos hit a 73% success rate. For context, no model could complete these at all before April 2025.

But AISI also offered a grounding perspective: Mythos can attack weakly-defended systems, but real-world networks with proper defenses, monitoring, and active defenders are a different game. Their recommendation wasn't "panic" — it was "patch your systems, update your access controls, and invest in logging."

Basically the same cybersecurity advice that's been given since 1997, just with more urgency.

## What We Actually Shipped

Let me step away from the drama and talk about what we built during this second week, because that's what actually matters for our users.

**PromptKit** got its biggest architecture overhaul since launch. The protocol-oriented template engine is faster, more testable, and handles streaming prompts without the weird stuttering that some users had reported. We shipped version 2.3 on Friday.

**Renovise** finally has working deep links on cold launch. This was the number one support request since January. The coordinator pattern Mythos suggested is also setting us up for the widget deep links we're planning for the next release.

**ThinkBud** got a comprehensive test suite for its sync engine — 43 test cases covering the concurrent save scenarios that Mythos flagged in week one. We also added a new export feature that Mythos helped us design: journal entries can now be exported as formatted PDFs with custom typography. We'd been putting this off because the `UIGraphicsPDFContext` API is fiddly. Mythos wrote the rendering pipeline in one pass, including proper text layout with paragraph spacing and pull quotes.

Three apps, meaningfully improved, in five working days. With an AI model that most developers can't access.

---

## The Bigger Picture

Here's what two weeks with Mythos Preview taught us:

**The gap between Opus 4.7 and Mythos is real, but it's not where you'd expect.** For everyday coding tasks — writing a new SwiftUI view, debugging a layout issue, generating boilerplate — Opus 4.7 and Mythos are functionally similar. The gap shows up in architectural reasoning. Mythos doesn't just solve the problem you asked about. It solves the problem you're going to have next week because of how you solved today's problem. That's a senior engineer behavior, and it's the hardest thing to teach.

**Access restrictions are creating a two-tier developer world.** The 40 companies in Project Glasswing have access to genuinely transformative tooling. Everyone else is working with models that are great but measurably less capable. And as the Fortune leak showed, the restriction doesn't even work.

**The competitive pressure is only going to accelerate.** GPT-5.5 dropping mid-week wasn't a coincidence. OpenAI saw the Mythos benchmarks and rushed to show they could compete. Within six months, models at this capability level will be commodity. The question is whether Anthropic will lead that race or just be remembered as the company that had it first and locked it away.

We're back to Opus 4.7 now. Our access window is closed. And honestly? Opus 4.7 is still phenomenal for daily [iOS development with Claude Code](/blog/vibe-coding-ios-comprehensive-analysis). We're shipping real apps, solving real problems, and building features our users actually want.

But we've seen what's coming. And it's going to change everything about how small teams build software.
