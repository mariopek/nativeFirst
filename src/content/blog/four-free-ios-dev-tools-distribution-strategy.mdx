Last week I had a choice. ThinkBud's growth had plateaued at a few hundred weekly actives, the WWDC content cycle was ending, and I was sitting on a half-finished blog draft with the working title "Why ThinkBud Is the Right Way to Study." I had three different angles for that post — flashcards as habit, spaced repetition done well, AI without the slop — and I couldn't get any of them to feel like they would actually convert anyone.

So I closed the draft. And instead I shipped four free developer tools in seven days.

This is why, what happened, and what I think it taught me about marketing iOS apps in 2026 as a one-person operation.

---

## The four tools

For context — these all live at [nativefirstapp.com/tools](https://nativefirstapp.com/tools), all four are free, client-side (your data never leaves the browser), no signup, no email gate, no "upgrade to pro" gimmick:

1. **[Privacy Manifest Generator](/tools/privacy-manifest-generator)** — answer 5 plain-English questions, get a valid `PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy` file ready to drop into Xcode. Apple started requiring these for new submissions; the official docs are 14 PDFs.
2. **[AASA Validator](/tools/aasa-validator)** — Apple's own AASA validator hard-fails on 3xx redirects and gives you a one-word error. This one follows redirects with a warning and tells you exactly what's broken in your `apple-app-site-association`.
3. **[App Icon Generator](/tools/app-icon-generator)** — iOS 26 added tinted and dark icon variants. Most generators don't handle them yet, or they handle the tinted variant wrong (the correct way is Rec. 709 luminance, which is what Apple does internally). This one does it right.
4. **[App Store Hero Maker](/tools/app-store-hero-maker)** — a Figma-style screenshot designer with 17 ready-made presets, live conversion guidance (headline length, contrast, conjunctions), bulk export to every required size as a ZIP. The free alternative to Mockuuups Studio ($99/yr) and AppLaunchpad ($30/mo).

Total build time: about 35 hours of focused work, spread across a week.

## Why this instead of writing about ThinkBud

I had two things going against the "just market the app harder" approach.

**The first is that everyone is doing that.** Open r/iOSProgramming on any given week and count the "I built [X], please check it out" posts. They get a polite nod and ten upvotes if the screenshot is pretty, and most of them disappear. Indie iOS dev marketing in 2026 is a saturated medium. The reader's instinct when they see another app launch post is to scroll.

**The second is that I genuinely don't know what part of ThinkBud sells it.** I have hypotheses. I think the spaced-repetition algorithm is sharper than Anki for typical-use-case decks. I think the AI card generation is faster than the alternatives because it runs on-device. I think the streak mechanics work because they're not gamified to death. But I don't know — and a marketing post that tries to sound certain about an unproven hypothesis comes off exactly the way it sounds. Bad.

Free tools sidestep both problems.

A free dev tool isn't pitching anything. It either works for the reader or it doesn't, and if it works, the reader saves an hour and remembers your name. The transactional nature of "here's a thing that fixes a thing you've been annoyed about" is unambiguous. There's no hype gap to bridge.

And the developer audience is the *right* audience for me. Every iOS developer who shipped an app this year has stared at a Privacy Manifest doc and groaned. Every one of them has lost an evening to a misconfigured AASA file. Every one has paid for App Store screenshot tooling that should have been free. These aren't sophisticated targeting decisions — they're table stakes annoyances I happen to have already solved for myself.

So the question wasn't "should I market my app?" The question was "do I have any leverage left in showing developers things that already exist on my hard drive?" Turns out yes.

## The wedge concept

There's a marketing term for this: a *wedge*. The idea is that you start with a narrow, free, low-friction offering that solves one specific pain, and then — once the user has met you, used your thing, and remembered your name — you have a much wider opening for the broader pitch.

The wedge isn't a trick. It's a sequence. Each tool kills one 30-minute annoyance. The user thinks "huh, that's nice, who made this?" and clicks the footer. The footer says "NativeFirst — we make free iOS dev tools and a few apps you might like." That's the entire pitch.

The reason this works is structural. A direct app pitch needs the user to make three decisions in a row: trust you, want what you're selling, and download it. A free tool needs them to make zero decisions — they're already on the page because they searched "App Store screenshot generator," and the tool either delivers or it doesn't. If it delivers, the trust comes for free. The "want your app" part is a separate visit, possibly weeks later, possibly never. That's fine — you've still earned a unit of attention that costs nothing to make.

I learned this framing this week from a Reddit comment, of all places. Someone on r/SideProject saw my "I built four free iOS tools" post and said "this is a nice wedge — free tools for dev pain pull way better than a broad app pitch." The fact that I had spent a week building toward that exact strategy without knowing the term for it made me realize I had been operating on instinct that someone smarter had already named.

## The math I'm betting on

Here's what I think happens over the next 90 days, with the specific numbers I'm tracking. I'm publishing them so I can come back in 3 months and tell you whether I was right.

**Top of funnel — search traffic.** The four tool pages target SEO keywords with combined estimated monthly volume around 4,000-8,000 searches. That's modest by mainstream standards, but extremely high-intent. Someone Googling "free PrivacyInfo.xcprivacy generator" is not a tire-kicker.

Realistic 90-day target: ~2,000 organic monthly visitors to the four tool pages combined. That's after the SEO clock starts ticking, which takes 6-12 weeks for a domain with NativeFirst's age and backlink profile (i.e. modest).

**Conversion to brand awareness.** Of those 2,000 monthly visitors, I estimate 15% will look at *anything else* on the site — read a blog post, click /apps, view the field notes. Conservative target: 300 monthly secondary page views from tool traffic.

**Conversion to actual users.** Of those 300, maybe 1-3% will end up downloading ThinkBud or RoleBud or Invoize. So 3-9 app downloads per month from the tool funnel. That sounds tiny. Compared to "zero direct downloads from a marketing blog post nobody read," it's infinity percent.

**The compounding play.** Tool pages don't decay the way blog posts do. A blog post titled "Swift 6.2 Concurrency Migration Guide" peaks in week one and trails off. A page titled "Free App Store Screenshot Generator" doesn't have an expiration. The math at month 3 is the same as the math at month 12, only the traffic has compounded.

If the 90-day numbers come in at half my estimate, the experiment was worth it. If they come in at 2x, this becomes the entire NativeFirst go-to-market strategy.

## What this approach is genuinely bad at

I want to be honest about the trade-offs because I have a brand to protect and "free tools fix everything" is a dishonest pitch.

**It doesn't move the needle on revenue this quarter.** The conversion path from tool user to paying app user is genuinely long. If I needed cash flow this month, I would not be building free tools — I would be running paid ads on Apple Search Ads for ThinkBud and accepting a $4 CPI to keep the funnel warm. The wedge approach is patient capital. It pays back in 6 months, not 6 days.

**It cannibalizes my time from app product work.** Every hour I spent on the App Store Hero Maker was an hour I didn't spend on ThinkBud's spaced-repetition algorithm, which is genuinely the part that needs more love. There's a version of me that could rationally argue I should have spent the week on the algorithm and not on the tool. I'm not sure which version is right.

**It signals a specific kind of brand.** "Free dev tools company" is a different positioning than "indie iOS apps company." I think they're complementary, but they're not identical. Some of the audience I'm building with the free tools will care about iOS development and never download ThinkBud, because they don't need a flashcard app. That's a real opportunity cost.

**The competition will catch up.** Once Mockuuups or AppLaunchpad notices a free competitor stealing search traffic, they have the option to either cut their pricing, build the conversion guidance feature themselves, or both. The window for being the *only* free option is probably 6-12 months. After that the differentiator has to be quality, design, and trust — none of which I can guarantee are stronger than a competitor with a 5-person team.

## What I'd tell another indie dev considering this

Three things, in order of conviction.

**One — pick tools that solve problems *you've actually had this year*.** Don't build a tool because it sounds like a clever wedge. Build it because you needed it for your own apps. The Privacy Manifest Generator, the AASA Validator, the Hero Maker — I built every one of them because I needed it for ThinkBud or Invoize first. The fact that they were also good wedges was a discovery, not a plan. If you build a tool you don't personally use, the polish gap shows in 30 seconds and the user never comes back.

**Two — bias toward shipping over polish.** My App Store Hero Maker has 17 presets. AppLaunchpad has 50+. I shipped anyway because 17 was enough to be useful for 80% of users, and I can add more presets in week 2 based on what people actually request. If I had waited until I had 50, I'd have shipped in July, and the WWDC season would be over.

**Three — make the tools' connection to your brand absolutely visible without being pushy.** Every tool page on NativeFirst has a tiny "Made by NativeFirst — we also make iOS apps. Check them out." in the footer. That's the entire pitch. If you make the connection too aggressive, the tool feels like a lead magnet (it is, but it shouldn't *feel* like one). If you hide the connection, you get the traffic and lose the brand-building. The middle is one polite line.
